How Credit Card Interest Works (And Why It's Expensive)

Credit cards typically charge daily compound interest on your average daily balance. This means interest accrues on your balance every single day, then that interest itself accrues more interest. This exponential growth is why credit card debt becomes so expensive so quickly.

Example: A $5,000 balance at 20% APR costs you about $83/month in interest alone. If you only pay $100/month, just $17 goes to principal while $83 covers interest. At this rate, it takes over 5 years to pay off $5,000 because you're mostly paying interest, not reducing the debt.

The minimum payment (usually 1-3% of your balance) is designed to keep you paying interest for as long as possible. Credit card companies profit most when you carry a balance and pay minimally. This is why paying more than the minimum is critical to escaping credit card debt.

Strategies to Eliminate Credit Card Debt

Avalanche Method (Best for total savings): Pay minimum on all cards, put all extra money toward the card with the highest APR. Once that's paid off, move to the next highest APR. This minimizes total interest paid.

Snowball Method (Best for motivation): Pay minimum on all cards, focus all extra payments on the smallest balance first. Once that's paid, move to the next smallest. Smaller, quicker wins can provide psychological motivation.

Balance Transfer: If you have decent credit, transfer your balance to a 0% APR card (usually 6-12 months promotional). This stops interest accrual temporarily, but watch out for balance transfer fees (3-5%).

Debt Consolidation Loan: Take a personal loan at a lower rate to pay off credit cards. Typical personal loan rates (8-12%) are much lower than credit cards (15-25%), saving thousands in interest.
