How Personal Loan Interest Works

Personal loans typically use a straightforward interest calculation method called amortization. Here's how it works: each month, your payment is calculated to pay down both the interest accrued since your last payment and a portion of the principal. Early in the loan term, most of your payment goes toward interest, but as time progresses, an increasing portion goes toward reducing the principal balance.

The formula used is: M = P × [r(1+r)^n] / [(1+r)^n - 1], where M is your monthly payment, P is the principal, r is your monthly interest rate, and n is the number of months. This ensures your payment remains fixed throughout the loan term, making budgeting predictable and manageable.

Unlike credit cards (where interest accrues daily on remaining balances), personal loans have fixed payments and a defined end date. This makes them ideal for consolidating debt, funding major purchases, or covering unexpected expenses without the uncertainty of variable interest rates.

What Is a Good Personal Loan Rate? (2026 Rates)

Personal loan interest rates vary significantly based on your credit score, income, employment history, and the lender you choose. As of April 2026, here's what borrowers can typically expect:

  • Excellent credit (750+): 6.5% – 10.5% APR
  • Good credit (700-749): 9% – 15% APR
  • Fair credit (650-699): 14% – 22% APR
  • Poor credit (below 650): 22% – 36%+ APR

If you have excellent credit and a stable income, you may qualify for rates under 10%. A difference of just 1-2% on a $10,000 loan can save you hundreds of dollars in interest over a 3-year period. This is why shopping around with multiple lenders and understanding your credit score is crucial before applying.
