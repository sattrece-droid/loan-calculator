Understanding Compound Interest: "The Eighth Wonder of the World"

Compound interest is interest earned not just on your original investment, but also on all previously earned interest. Albert Einstein famously called it "the eighth wonder of the world" because of its incredible growth potential over time.

Here's a simple example: If you deposit $10,000 at 5% annual interest, after year one you earn $500, giving you $10,500. In year two, you earn 5% on the new total ($10,500), which is $525, not just $500. This means your interest is compounding, earning interest on itself. The longer your money stays invested, the more dramatic this effect becomes.

The mathematical formula is: A = P(1 + r/n)^(nt), where A is the future amount, P is principal, r is annual rate, n is compounding frequency, and t is time in years. Even small increases in interest rate or time horizon can result in dramatically larger final balances due to compounding.

Why Compounding Frequency Matters

How often your interest is compounded significantly affects your final balance. Here's why:

  • Daily compounding: Interest is calculated and added 365 times per year, maximizing growth. High-yield savings accounts often offer this.
  • Monthly compounding: Interest is added 12 times yearly. Common for many savings accounts and certificates of deposit (CDs).
  • Quarterly compounding: Interest compounds 4 times per year. Less common for retail banking products.
  • Annual compounding: Interest is added just once per year. Slowest growth, typical for some bonds or older accounts.

On a $10,000 investment at 5% interest over 10 years, daily compounding yields approximately $16,487, while annual compounding yields $16,289. That $200 difference comes purely from how frequently interest compounds. For larger amounts or longer periods, these differences multiply substantially.

Where Can You Get Good Compound Interest Rates?

As of April 2026, here are typical rates offered:

  • High-yield savings accounts: 4.0% – 5.2% APY with daily compounding
  • Money market accounts: 3.8% – 5.0% APY
  • Certificates of deposit (CDs): 4.5% – 5.5% APY (depending on term length)
  • Regular savings accounts: 0.01% – 0.5% APY (much lower)
  • Stock market investments: Historical average 10% annually (but with volatility and risk)

Shopping around for better rates is crucial. A 1% difference in APY might seem small, but on $50,000 over 20 years, it equals thousands in additional earnings. Use our calculator to see the impact different rates have on your savings goals.
