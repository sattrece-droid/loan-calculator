What is Simple Interest? How Does it Work?

Simple interest is the most straightforward method of calculating interest. It's calculated only on the principal amount, not on previously accumulated interest. The formula is: I = P × R × T, where I is interest, P is principal, R is the annual rate (as a decimal), and T is time in years.

Example: If you lend $1,000 at 5% simple interest for 3 years, you earn exactly $150 in interest ($1,000 × 0.05 × 3), regardless of how much the balance has grown. You always calculate interest only on the original $1,000.

Simple interest is commonly used for short-term loans, auto loans (which use amortization, but the interest calculation is similar), and some savings products. It's easy to understand and predict because the amount earned or paid never changes, making it ideal for budgeting.

Simple Interest vs. Compound Interest: Which Is Better?

The key difference: Simple interest is calculated only on principal; compound interest is calculated on principal plus previously earned interest.

  • Simple Interest (For borrowers): Better because you pay less total interest. Example: $1,000 at 5% for 3 years = $150 interest.
  • Compound Interest (For savers): Better because your money grows faster. Example: $1,000 at 5% annually compounded for 3 years = $157.63 interest.

For short periods (under 2-3 years), the difference is small. For longer periods, compound interest dramatically outpaces simple interest. That's why CDs and savings accounts emphasize APY (which includes compounding) over APR. As a borrower, simple interest saves you money; as an investor, compound interest works in your favor.
