What is an Amortization Schedule? Understanding Your Loan Breakdown

An amortization schedule is a detailed table showing every payment on a loan throughout its life. Each row includes: payment number, payment amount, principal portion, interest portion, and remaining balance. It's called "amortization" because you're systematically paying down the loan from "mort" (death) and "amort" (principal)—literally paying the debt to death.

Example amortization (simplified) on a $10,000 loan at 6% for 36 months ($299/month):

  • Month 1: Payment $299 = $50 interest + $249 principal. Balance: $9,751
  • Month 2: Payment $299 = $49 interest + $250 principal. Balance: $9,501
  • Month 36: Payment $299 = $1.50 interest + $297.50 principal. Balance: $0

Notice how interest starts high ($50) and declines as the balance decreases. This is why early payoff feels so impactful—you're finally paying down principal instead of funding the lender's earnings.

How Extra Payments Transform Your Loan Timeline & Savings

Extra payments disproportionately impact your loan because the additional funds go directly to principal (your minimum payment covers interest first). Even small extra payments compound dramatically over time.

Example impact of $100/month extra on a $300,000, 30-year mortgage at 7%:

  • Regular payment only: Payoff in 360 months (30 years), total interest: ~$419,000
  • With $100/month extra: Payoff in ~298 months (24.8 years), total interest: ~$346,000
  • Savings: 5.2 years faster + $73,000 in interest saved!

This is why making one extra payment per year (dividing your monthly payment by 12 and paying that amount bi-weekly) saves years off your loan. The earlier you start, the more you benefit.

How Amortization Schedules Help You Plan

Benefits of understanding your amortization schedule:

  • See the true cost: Many people don't realize they'll pay $400K+ interest on a $300K mortgage. Seeing it in the schedule motivates extra payments.
  • Plan for payoff: Know exactly when you'll own your home/car free and clear.
  • Tax planning: Mortgage interest is tax-deductible. Your schedule shows how much interest you paid for tax purposes.
  • Refinancing decisions: Compare refinancing options side-by-side using different schedules to see if it's worth the fees.
  • Extra payment strategy: See how different extra payment amounts affect payoff dates and total interest saved.
